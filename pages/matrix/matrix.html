2D Matrix Rotations
Proof

A position in a two-dimensional space can be given in cartesian coordinates (x,y) or in polar coordinates (r, φ). Cartesian coordinates specify each point by a set of coordinates whereas with polar coordinates, each point is determined by a distance from a reference point (the origin) and an angle. Suppose that a position is rotated by the angle θ:

The new vector’s polar coordinates are (r, φ + θ). The new cartesian coordinates can be calculated using the trigonometric identities for the sum of two angles:

\[ \begin{align*} x_1 &= r\cos(\phi+\theta) \\ &= r(\cos\phi \cos\theta - \sin\phi \sin\theta) \\ &= (r\cos\phi)\cos(\theta) - (r\sin\phi)\sin\theta \\ &= x\cos\theta - y\sin(\theta) \\ \end{align*} \] \[ \begin{align*} y_1 &= r\sin(\phi+\theta) \\ &= r(\sin\phi \cos\theta + \cos\phi \sin\theta) \\ &= (r\sin\phi)\cos(\theta) + (r\cos\phi)\sin\theta \\ &= y\cos\theta + x\sin(\theta) \\ &= x\sin\theta + y\cos(\theta) \end{align*} \]

The transformation of the position (x,y) to the position (x1,y1) caused by a rotation through the angle θ can be expressed in matrix notation as:

\[\begin{bmatrix} x\\y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ y_1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} cos(\theta)&-sin(\theta) \\ sin(\theta)&cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix}\]
